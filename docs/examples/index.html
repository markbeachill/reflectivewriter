Examples

What a session looks like

Short, illustrative exchanges. Real sessions are interactive: the tutor asks, you answer in your own words, and it responds to what you actually wrote. The tutor never fills in your experience for you.

Moving from description to meaning

You paste (into RF2, So-What Deepener)

We had a difficult team meeting about the new rota. I said my bit and then it ended. I felt annoyed.

The tutor replies

Good — there's a real feeling there to work with. "Annoyed" is the start, not the end. So what was underneath it: not being heard, a fairness issue, worry about the workload, something else? Pick the most honest one and finish this sentence in your own words: "It mattered to me because…"

Notice it does not tell you why you were annoyed. It points to the gap and asks you to fill it.

Applying a framework without box-ticking

You paste (into FW1, Gibbs)

Conclusion: I have learned I need better communication skills.

The tutor replies

That conclusion would fit almost any reflection, which is a sign it is too general. Gibbs' conclusion asks what you now understand from this situation that you did not before. What specifically about your communication did this event reveal — and in what kind of moment? Try: "What I now see is that when…, I tend to…"

The tutor coaches a genuine, specific conclusion rather than writing one.

An anonymisation check (specialist library)

You paste (into NH4, Anonymisation and Confidentiality Check)

Last Tuesday on the early shift, my mentor Sarah and I looked after a 4-year-old boy with a rare metabolic condition in bed 3.

The tutor replies

Several details here could identify real people: a named colleague, a specific day and shift, a child's exact age, a rare condition, and a bed number. A rare condition plus a small age range can be especially identifying. Removing only the name would not be enough. Try something like: "I worked with my practice supervisor to care for a young child with a complex long-term condition." Keep the day, shift and bed out entirely.

The check explains why each detail is a risk, so you learn to anonymise your own writing in future.

When you are stuck

You type

I'm stuck. I don't even know what I felt.

The tutor replies

That's fine, and it's common. Let's make it smaller. Think back to the moment itself — not what you should have felt, just the body and the thought at the time. Was there a flicker of something: relief, dread, frustration, pride, blankness? You don't have to name it perfectly. Give me one rough word and we'll work outward from there.

"I'm stuck" always lowers the bar and offers a smaller next step. It never writes the answer for you.
